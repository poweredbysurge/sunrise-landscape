Snow Plowing vs. Snow Removal: Costs, Differences, and What to Expect

Most people use the terms interchangeably. A snowstorm hits, they call a contractor, and whatever shows up to clear the driveway gets called "snow removal." But snow plowing and snow removal are genuinely different services with different equipment, different pricing structures, and different results. Knowing which one you actually need prevents a bill that surprises you and a property that still isn't clear the way you expected.

This guide explains what separates the two services, what each costs in Northern Virginia, and how to decide what's right for your property before the next storm hits.

Key Takeaways
  • Snow plowing moves snow to the sides of your driveway or lot. Snow removal hauls it away entirely.
  • Most residential properties only need plowing. Snow removal is typically for properties where accumulated piles create safety or access issues.
  • Northern Virginia residential plowing runs $50–$150 per visit depending on driveway size and storm volume.
  • Snow removal adds $200–$800+ per visit due to the equipment and hauling required.
  • Seasonal contracts offer predictable pricing and priority service. Per-event billing costs more per storm but requires no commitment.

What Is Snow Plowing?

Snow plowing uses a truck-mounted or tractor-mounted blade to push snow off paved surfaces and pile it along the edges. The snow stays on your property — pushed to the sides of your driveway, to the corners of a parking lot, or along the edges of a walkway cleared by a snowblower.

Plowing is fast, cost-effective, and appropriate for the vast majority of Northern Virginia residential properties. A standard residential driveway can be cleared in 10 to 20 minutes depending on length and layout. The piles left along the edges melt naturally once temperatures rise, which in Northern Virginia typically happens within a few days of any given storm.

Plowing limitations become relevant when snow volume is high, when your property has limited space to pile snow, or when accumulated piles from multiple storms start blocking sightlines or encroaching on walkways. That's when snow removal becomes worth considering.

What Is Snow Removal?

Snow removal means physically hauling accumulated snow off your property. This requires a front-end loader or bobcat to scoop the piles and a dump truck or large truck with a trailer to transport the snow off-site. It is substantially more expensive than plowing because it requires more equipment, more manpower, and more time per visit.

Snow removal makes sense when:

  • Multiple storms have built up piles that are blocking parking spaces or fire lanes
  • Your commercial property has no room to push snow without losing parking capacity
  • Snow piles have become ice mounds that won't melt before the next storm
  • Sightlines at driveways or intersections are dangerously blocked
  • HOA rules or municipal regulations require clear sidewalks and curbs

For most residential properties in Great Falls, McLean, Vienna, and Oakton, full snow removal is rarely needed after a single storm. It becomes relevant after significant accumulation from back-to-back storms, or when a pile has been compacted and partially refrozen into something a plow can no longer move efficiently.

Snow Plowing vs. Snow Removal Costs in Northern Virginia

Pricing varies by property size, storm intensity, contractor, and whether you're on a seasonal contract or calling per event. Here are realistic ranges for the Northern Virginia market.

Residential Snow Plowing

  • Short driveway (1–2 car widths, under 50 feet): $50–$80 per visit
  • Standard driveway (2–3 car widths, 50–100 feet): $75–$125 per visit
  • Long or circular driveway (100+ feet, multiple cars): $100–$175 per visit
  • Sidewalk and walkway clearing (by hand or snowblower): $25–$75 additional
  • Salting/de-icing: $30–$75 additional depending on area treated

Commercial Snow Plowing

  • Small lot (under 15,000 sq ft): $75–$200 per visit
  • Medium lot (15,000–50,000 sq ft): $175–$450 per visit
  • Large lot (50,000+ sq ft): $400–$1,200+ per visit

Snow Removal Costs

  • Residential (average-size pile haul): $250–$600 per event
  • Commercial (per load hauled off-site): $300–$800+ per load depending on equipment and distance
  • Full commercial lot clearing with haul-off: Can exceed $2,000–$5,000+ for large properties with significant accumulation

Seasonal Contract vs. Per-Event Billing

This is the decision that has the biggest impact on your total cost over a winter season.

Seasonal Contracts

A seasonal contract covers an agreed number of events or an entire season for a flat fee. You pay the same amount whether Northern Virginia gets two storms or twelve. In a heavy snow year, this is a significant savings. In a light year, the contractor comes out ahead.

The real advantage of seasonal contracts beyond cost is priority placement. When a storm hits, contractors work their seasonal clients first. Per-event callers wait. In Northern Virginia, where storms frequently hit on weekday mornings before the commute, priority response can mean the difference between making it out on time or being stuck until mid-afternoon.

Residential seasonal contracts in Northern Virginia typically run $400–$900 for the season. Commercial contracts vary widely based on lot size and service scope.

Per-Event Billing

Per-event pricing means you pay only for services rendered. This can feel like the safer choice, but it has real downsides. You're a lower priority than seasonal clients. Rates per storm are higher. And in a heavy winter, per-event costs add up quickly. If Northern Virginia gets eight plowable storms in a winter, per-event billing at $100 per visit is $800 — more than most seasonal contracts would have cost.

Per-event makes sense if you travel frequently and your property sits empty for much of the winter, or if you have a backup plan and don't need guaranteed response time.

What About Ice Management?

Ice is a separate conversation from snow. Black ice, refrozen slush, and compacted snow that has glazed over after a temperature drop all require treatment beyond plowing. Salt, calcium chloride, and sand are the three most common options, and they have different effective temperature ranges and different effects on your hardscape and landscaping.

Rock Salt (Sodium Chloride)

The cheapest and most common option. Effective down to about 15°F. The downside for Northern Virginia homeowners with nice landscaping: salt runoff damages grass, ornamental plants, and shrubs along driveways. It also accelerates deterioration of concrete surfaces over time. If you've invested in professional planting beds along your driveway, be cautious about heavy salt application near them.

Calcium Chloride

More expensive but effective at much lower temperatures (down to -25°F). It works faster and requires less product to achieve the same result. Less damaging to plant material when used correctly. A better choice for homeowners with significant hardscape investments or landscaping adjacent to treated surfaces.

Sand

Provides traction without melting ice. Environmentally gentler and harmless to plants. The downside is cleanup — sand tracks into the house, clogs storm drains, and requires sweeping up in spring. Best used in moderation for targeted traction on walkways or steps rather than full driveway treatment.

Protecting Your Landscaping During Snow Management

Snow plowing and ice treatment can damage your landscape if not handled carefully. Hardscape investments — natural stone driveways, brick pavers, ornamental borders — are particularly vulnerable to aggressive plowing angles and salt overspray.

A few things to communicate clearly to any snow contractor you hire:

  • Identify the boundaries of any planting beds adjacent to paved surfaces
  • Note any irrigation heads, landscape lighting fixtures, or edging materials that could be damaged by plow contact
  • Specify where snow piles should be pushed — especially on properties where piling snow on specific areas could suffocate plants
  • Request calcium chloride over rock salt if you have significant landscape investment along treated surfaces

When Should You Get on a Snow Route?

Northern Virginia snow routes fill quickly in November. Contractors have a limited number of properties they can service within their response time window, and once those slots are full, new clients go on a waitlist or to per-event response.

If you want guaranteed seasonal service and priority response, the time to get on the list is before the first forecasted storm — ideally by October. Waiting until the first snow is in the forecast means calling during the busiest, highest-stress 24 hours in a snow contractor's year. Response is slower, availability is limited, and prices may reflect the urgency.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Snow Service

  • What triggers a service event — a minimum accumulation threshold? A specific forecast?
  • What is your typical response window after a storm ends?
  • Do you salt automatically or only on request?
  • How do you handle re-freeze — does the contract include a return visit?
  • What is your policy for storms that happen overnight versus during business hours?
  • Are you licensed and insured in Virginia?

A contractor who can answer these questions clearly and specifically before you sign anything is one worth working with.
