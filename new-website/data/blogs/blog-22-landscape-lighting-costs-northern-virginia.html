Landscape Lighting Cost, Design Tips, and What Northern Virginia Homeowners Should Know

Landscape lighting is one of the few outdoor investments that changes how you experience your property every single day. Not just curb appeal at noon — the way your home looks at 9pm when you pull into the driveway, the way your backyard feels on a summer evening, the security that comes from a well-lit property at night. It works around the clock in a way that a new planting bed or patio only partially does.

The challenge most homeowners run into is budgeting for it. Landscape lighting estimates vary enormously — from $500 DIY kits at the hardware store to professional installations that run $15,000 or more. Understanding what drives that range helps you set realistic expectations and make a decision that matches both your property and your goals.

Key Takeaways
  • Professional landscape lighting in Northern Virginia typically costs $2,500–$15,000+ depending on scope.
  • The main cost drivers are the number of fixtures, fixture quality, transformer capacity, and the complexity of the wiring run.
  • LED fixtures cost more upfront but pay back within 2–3 years through energy savings versus halogen.
  • Uplighting on trees and architectural features, path lighting, and step lighting are the highest-ROI lighting applications.
  • Properly installed low-voltage landscape lighting integrates with smart home systems and can be controlled by phone or timer.

How Much Does Landscape Lighting Cost in Northern Virginia?

There is no single answer, but here are honest ranges based on what professional installation looks like in the Fairfax and Loudoun County market.

Entry-Level System (6–12 Fixtures)

A focused system for a front yard — path lighting along a walkway, two or three uplights on key trees or the house facade, and step lighting at the entry. This scope uses a single transformer and a straightforward wiring run.

Typical cost: $2,500–$5,000 installed

This range assumes quality commercial-grade brass or copper fixtures, a programmable transformer with photocell and timer capability, and professional installation with proper burial depth and weatherproofing at all connections.

Mid-Range System (12–25 Fixtures)

A complete front and backyard system — path lights, uplights on focal trees, downlighting from trees or structures, and accent lighting on garden beds or hardscape features. Multiple transformer zones allow independent control of different areas.

Typical cost: $5,000–$10,000 installed

Comprehensive System (25+ Fixtures)

Full property coverage for larger lots common in Great Falls, McLean, and Oakton. Includes front entry, driveway, backyard entertainment areas, pool or water feature lighting, and specimen tree uplighting. May include integration with home automation systems.

Typical cost: $10,000–$20,000+ installed

What Drives Landscape Lighting Cost?

Number and Quality of Fixtures

This is the biggest variable. Budget fixtures from big-box stores run $15–$40 each and often fail within two to three seasons due to moisture intrusion, UV degradation, or poorly cast metals. Professional-grade brass, copper, or aluminum fixtures run $80–$300+ per unit and are warranted to last decades with minimal maintenance.

The math on fixture quality is straightforward. Buying cheap fixtures and replacing them every three years costs more than buying quality fixtures once. And every replacement means a new installation labor cost on top of the fixture cost.

Transformer Capacity and Zones

The transformer converts your home's 120V current to 12V low-voltage power for the fixtures. Transformer capacity is measured in wattage. A small system might use a 150-watt transformer. A large system with multiple zones might require 600 watts or more across multiple transformers.

Transformers run $150–$600+ depending on capacity, brand, and features. Smart transformers with Wi-Fi connectivity and app control add cost but allow you to set schedules, dim individual zones, and integrate with Alexa or Google Home without additional hardware.

Wiring Complexity and Burial

Low-voltage landscape wire must be properly buried to avoid damage from foot traffic, lawnmowers, and frost heaving. The National Electrical Code and Virginia building requirements specify minimum burial depths. Longer wire runs, rocky soil, and properties with existing hardscape that requires wire routing underneath add labor time and cost.

Properties in Great Falls and McLean — where mature trees have extensive root systems — require careful wire routing to avoid root damage and to ensure the system doesn't surface-heave over time.

LED vs. Halogen

The industry has almost entirely transitioned to LED. Halogen fixtures run at 10–35 watts each. LED equivalents run at 2–8 watts and produce equivalent or superior light output. On a 20-fixture system, the difference in operating cost is $150–$300 per year.

LED fixtures cost slightly more upfront but pay back the difference within two to three years. Beyond payback, they last 50,000 hours versus 2,000 hours for halogen — meaning far less bulb replacement over the life of the system.

Which Landscape Lighting Types Give the Best Return?

Uplighting on Trees and Architecture

Directing light upward into the canopy of a mature oak or magnolia — or washing light up the facade of your home — creates dramatic contrast that elevates the entire property's curb appeal. This is the application that most dramatically changes how a property looks at night, and it's the first thing guests and neighbors notice.

For Northern Virginia properties with mature specimen trees, uplighting is almost always worth the investment. A single well-placed uplight on a 40-year-old oak creates an effect no other landscaping element can replicate.

Path and Walkway Lighting

Path lighting serves both aesthetic and safety functions. A well-lit entry walkway guides guests, reduces trip hazards, and signals a well-maintained property. From an ROI perspective, path lighting along a primary walkway offers some of the best return because it's visible to anyone approaching the front of the home and functions every night of the year.

Step and Grade Lighting

Steps and grade changes present real safety risks in the dark. Integrating lighting into risers, retaining walls, or landscape borders adjacent to steps is both functional and refined. It's also one of the finishing details that separates a professionally designed lighting system from a DIY kit.

Downlighting from Trees

Mounting fixtures high in mature trees and directing light downward creates a moonlighting effect — soft, natural-feeling light that falls across patios, seating areas, and garden beds. This is popular in higher-end residential installations and works particularly well for backyard entertainment spaces where you want ambient light without the harshness of a wall-mounted fixture.

What Does Professional Installation Include?

When Sunrise Landscape and Design installs a lighting system, the process includes a site walk to identify the right fixture locations, a design proposal showing fixture placement and expected light patterns, professional installation with proper burial and waterproofing, transformer setup with timer and photocell programming, and a walkthrough at dusk so you can see the system in operation and make any adjustments before we leave.

A quality installation also includes a service plan — fixtures get repositioned as trees and plantings grow, transformers require seasonal adjustment as the angle of available daylight shifts, and occasional fixture replacements are a normal part of long-term system care.

Landscape Lighting and Smart Home Integration

Modern low-voltage landscape lighting systems integrate cleanly with most smart home platforms. The transformer is typically the point of control — a Wi-Fi-enabled transformer allows you to set schedules, dim zones independently, create seasonal presets, and trigger lighting remotely. Lutron, Kichler, and FX Luminaire offer professional-grade systems with robust app and voice control support.

Smart integration is especially useful for properties in Northern Virginia where sunrise and sunset times shift significantly across the seasons. A smart transformer adjusts automatically rather than requiring you to manually reprogram the timer twice a year.

Landscape Lighting Maintenance

Professional-grade systems require minimal ongoing maintenance. Annual service typically involves cleaning fixture lenses (oxidation and debris accumulate over time), checking and tightening wire connections, repositioning fixtures that have shifted due to frost or mulch settling, and replacing any failed lamps.

Most contractors offer an annual service agreement for $150–$400 depending on system size. If you have a larger investment in your lighting system, this is worth the peace of mind.

Is Landscape Lighting Worth the Investment?

For most Northern Virginia homeowners, yes. The combination of security value, curb appeal, and the daily quality-of-life improvement from a beautifully lit property in the evening makes landscape lighting one of the more satisfying outdoor investments you can make.

The properties in Great Falls, McLean, and Oakton with the strongest curb appeal after dark almost always have a professionally designed and installed lighting system. When done well, it's not something you consciously notice — it's just that the property looks right.
