Credentials and Licensing

In Virginia, landscape contractors must hold a Class A or Class B contractor license for projects above certain dollar thresholds. Ask any company you're considering whether they are licensed in Virginia and ask to see proof. A company unwilling to share this information before you've signed anything is not a company you want to hire.

Local Portfolio

Ask to see completed projects in your area. Not stock photos. Not renderings. Actual finished work from neighborhoods like Oakton, Ashburn, or Leesburg. A company with deep roots in Northern Virginia should be able to show you before-and-after documentation of projects similar in scope to yours without hesitation.

References You Can Actually Call

Request two or three client references and call them. Ask specifically about communication during the project, whether the timeline was honored, and whether the finished result matched what was promised. Those three questions will tell you almost everything you need to know about how a company operates.

What Are the Red Flags to Watch for When Hiring a Landscape Design Company?

Some warning signs appear before work even begins. Learning to spot them early saves you from a painful experience later.

Vague or Verbal Proposals

A professional landscape design company puts everything in writing. Scope of work, materials, timeline, payment schedule, and what happens if something changes mid-project. If a contractor is offering you a verbal agreement or a one-paragraph email as a contract, walk away. Ambiguity benefits the contractor, not you.

Large Upfront Deposits

Requiring a reasonable deposit to secure your spot and cover initial design costs is standard. Requiring 50% or more of the total project cost before a single plant goes in the ground is not. This is the pattern that leaves homeowners with a half-finished yard and a contractor who has stopped answering the phone.

No Design Process

The design phase is where a qualified company earns your trust. A professional firm will walk you through concept options, show you how your yard will look before installation begins, and give you the opportunity to refine the vision before anything is built. A company that skips this step or treats it as an afterthought is prioritizing speed over quality.

Slow or Inconsistent Communication

How a company communicates before you hire them is a direct preview of how they'll communicate during your project. If calls go unreturned for days, if questions get vague answers, if no one follows up after an initial meeting, those patterns will not improve once they have your deposit.

How Do You Evaluate Landscape Design Services Before Committing?

A first consultation with a reputable landscape company should feel like a professional conversation, not a sales pitch. The company should ask questions about how you use your outdoor space, what you want to change, what your timeline looks like, and what your priorities are. A company that leads with pricing before understanding your project is not a company that does thorough work.

Pay attention to whether they mention specific site conditions. Northern Virginia's clay-heavy soil creates drainage challenges that affect planting choices, grading decisions, and hardscape longevity. A company that doesn't bring this up in an initial consultation either hasn't noticed or doesn't consider it worth mentioning. Neither is a good sign.

What Questions Should You Ask a Landscape Design Company Near You?

Going into a consultation with the right questions puts you in control of the conversation and gives you the information you need to make a confident decision.

Questions about experience and process:

  • How long have you been operating in Northern Virginia specifically?
  • Can you walk me through your design process from first meeting to project completion?
  • How do you handle changes to scope or unexpected site conditions?
  • Who will be my point of contact during the project, and how often will I receive updates?

Questions about the project itself:

  • Have you worked on properties with similar grades, drainage issues, or HOA requirements?
  • What materials do you recommend for this project and why?
  • What does your payment schedule look like, and what does it correspond to in terms of project milestones?
  • What warranty do you offer on your work and on the plant material?

A company that answers these questions confidently and specifically, without deflecting or getting defensive, is a company worth considering. A company that gives you vague answers or dismisses the questions as unnecessary is one to avoid.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if a landscape design company near me is licensed in Virginia?

You can verify a contractor's license through the Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR) database at dpor.virginia.gov. Any company doing work above $1,000 in Virginia must hold a valid contractor's license. Ask for their license number before signing anything.

How many estimates should I get before hiring a landscape design company?

Getting two to three estimates is reasonable for larger projects. More than that often creates confusion rather than clarity. Focus less on comparing prices and more on comparing the depth of the proposals, the quality of the process each company describes, and how you feel about the communication in the initial consultation.

What's the difference between a landscape designer and a landscape architect?

Landscape architects hold a licensed professional degree and can stamp engineering drawings for permitted work like retaining walls and grading. Landscape designers typically focus on planting design and outdoor living layouts. For most residential projects in Northern Virginia, a qualified landscape design firm with in-house design-build capabilities covers both needs without requiring a separate architect.

How long does a typical landscape design project take in Northern Virginia?

The design phase for a residential project takes two to four weeks. Installation timelines vary from one week for smaller projects to two to three months for large design-build projects with hardscape, drainage, and full planting plans. Permitting in Fairfax or Loudoun County can add time for projects requiring structural approvals.

Can I hire a landscape design company just for the design, then hire someone else to install?

Yes, though this approach often creates problems. The company that designed the project understands the intent of every decision. A different installer working from plans they didn't create may make substitutions or shortcuts that compromise the finished result. For best outcomes, working with a single design-build firm from start to finish is the more reliable path.
