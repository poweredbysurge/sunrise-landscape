What Does Commercial Landscape Maintenance Actually Include?

Commercial landscape maintenance covers far more than mowing. A Lawn & Landscape Industry Survey (2024) found that the average commercial maintenance contract includes 12-15 distinct service categories. The scope depends on property type, but most programs share a core set of recurring tasks.

Core Maintenance Services

The foundation of any commercial landscape maintenance program includes weekly or biweekly mowing, edging, and blowing during the growing season. In Northern Virginia, that season runs roughly from early April through late October. Turf care, including fertilization and weed control, typically follows a 6-8 application schedule.

Beyond turf, expect pruning of shrubs and ornamental trees, mulch installation (usually twice per year), and leaf removal in fall. Irrigation system management is critical for Reston Town Center properties and Tysons Corner commercial complexes where extensive planting beds demand consistent water delivery.

Enhanced Services

Properties seeking a premium appearance add seasonal color rotations (spring annuals, summer tropicals, fall mums), ornamental lighting maintenance, and hardscape cleaning. Snow and ice management is also bundled into many Northern Virginia commercial contracts since winters here are unpredictable.

How Should You Schedule Maintenance Throughout the Year?

Seasonal timing is everything in Northern Virginia. The Virginia Cooperative Extension recommends aligning landscape maintenance with the region's USDA Hardiness Zone 7a climate, which brings hot, humid summers and variable winters. A structured four-season schedule prevents costly reactive repairs.

Virginia Cooperative Extension recommends that commercial properties in USDA Zone 7a follow a four-season maintenance calendar, with turf fertilization beginning in early March and final winterization wrapping up by late November.

Spring (March - May)

Spring is your highest-activity season. Tasks include irrigation system startup, pre-emergent herbicide application, first mowing cycle, mulch installation, and annual flower planting. For Ashburn and Brambleton HOA common areas, spring is also when you should schedule tree inspections after winter storm damage.

Budget roughly 30% of your annual maintenance spend for spring. It sets the tone for the rest of the year.

Summer (June - August)

Summer shifts to a sustaining rhythm. Weekly mowing, irrigation monitoring, and pest management are the primary tasks. Heat stress is a real concern for fescue lawns in the Dulles corridor, where afternoon temperatures regularly exceed 95 degrees. Mowing heights should increase to 3.5-4 inches during peak heat.

Summer is also when commercial properties replace spring annuals with heat-tolerant varieties. Expect 25% of your annual budget here.

Fall (September - November)

Fall maintenance is often underestimated. Aeration and overseeding in September are critical for fescue-dominant properties across Fairfax and Loudoun counties. Leaf removal can be a massive task for properties with mature tree canopies, particularly in Great Falls and McLean.

Fall is also when you should schedule hardscape repairs, drainage checks, and irrigation winterization. This season accounts for about 25% of annual spend.

Winter (December - February)

Winter maintenance in Northern Virginia centers on snow and ice management, holiday lighting removal, and equipment inspections. Dormant pruning of deciduous trees and shrubs should happen in January or February.

For properties along the Dulles Toll Road corridor, having a snow removal contract in place by October is essential. Late contracting often means higher prices and lower priority.

How Do You Choose the Right Commercial Landscape Maintenance Vendor?

Choosing the wrong vendor costs more than the contract itself. The International Facility Management Association (IFMA) reports that switching landscape vendors mid-contract increases annual maintenance costs by 20-30% due to transition inefficiencies. Selecting the right partner from the start protects both your budget and your property's appearance.

The International Facility Management Association reports that switching landscape vendors mid-contract increases annual maintenance costs by 20 to 30 percent due to transition inefficiencies, making initial vendor selection critical for property managers.

Evaluation Criteria

Start with non-negotiable requirements. Any commercial landscape maintenance company should carry a minimum of $1 million in general liability insurance and $500,000 in workers' compensation coverage. In Virginia, verify that the vendor holds a Virginia Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (VDACS) pesticide business license if they're applying any chemicals.

Beyond insurance, evaluate these factors:

  • Experience with your property type: An HOA maintenance specialist may not be the right fit for a Class A office building, and vice versa
  • Crew consistency: Ask whether the same crew will service your property weekly
  • Communication protocols: How quickly do they respond to urgent issues like fallen trees or irrigation breaks?
  • Equipment quality: Commercial-grade equipment matters for large properties
  • References from similar properties: Ask for three references from properties of comparable size and type

Red Flags to Watch For

Be cautious of vendors who won't provide a detailed scope of work, those who significantly underbid competitors (they'll cut corners or request change orders), and companies that can't show proof of current insurance. Also watch for vendors who don't conduct a thorough site walk before quoting.

: Property managers often evaluate vendors purely on price per visit. But the real differentiator is response time. A vendor who can mobilize a crew within 4 hours after a storm does more for tenant satisfaction than one who saves you $200 per month.

A vague contract is the number one source of disputes between property managers and landscape vendors. The Community Associations Institute (CAI) found that 62% of HOA board complaints about landscape vendors stem from unclear scope definitions, not poor workmanship. Your contract needs to be specific.

Per-Visit vs. Annual Contracts

Per-visit contracts charge a set rate each time crews arrive. They offer flexibility but make budgeting unpredictable. Snow removal often operates on a per-visit or per-event basis.

Annual contracts bundle all services into 12 monthly payments. They typically save 10-15% compared to per-visit pricing because vendors can plan labor and materials more efficiently. For most Northern Virginia commercial properties, annual contracts are the better choice.

Some property managers use a hybrid approach: an annual contract for core maintenance with per-visit pricing for snow removal and emergency services. This structure works well for Tysons Corner office complexes where snow frequency varies dramatically year to year.

Essential Contract Clauses

Every commercial landscape maintenance contract should include:

  1. Detailed scope of work with visit frequency for each service
  2. Performance standards (mowing height, response times, seasonal plant health benchmarks)
  3. Insurance requirements with proof of renewal
  4. Termination clause (30-60 day notice is standard)
  5. Price escalation limits (cap annual increases at 3-5%)
  6. Safety and compliance requirements
  7. Communication and reporting schedule

What Compliance and Safety Standards Apply to Commercial Landscaping?

Commercial landscape maintenance involves real liability. The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) reports that the landscaping and groundskeeping industry has an injury rate of 5.2 per 100 full-time workers, higher than the national average for all industries. Property managers share liability when vendors don't follow safety protocols.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports a 5.2 injury rate per 100 full-time workers in landscaping and groundskeeping, above the national average, making safety compliance a shared liability concern for property managers and their vendors.

Virginia-Specific Requirements

In Virginia, commercial landscape vendors must hold a VDACS pesticide business license for any chemical applications. The Virginia Occupational Safety and Health (VOSH) program enforces workplace safety standards that mirror federal OSHA rules. Property managers should verify that vendors maintain current compliance with both.

For properties near waterways or in Loudoun County's environmentally sensitive areas, stormwater management regulations affect how landscaping crews handle fertilizer runoff and erosion control. The Northern Virginia Soil and Water Conservation District provides guidance specific to the region.

ADA and Accessibility

Commercial properties must maintain ADA-compliant pathways. That means landscape maintenance crews need to keep walkways clear, trim vegetation away from signage, and ensure that mulch or debris doesn't obstruct accessible routes. Failure to maintain these standards creates legal exposure.

How Can You Make Commercial Landscape Maintenance More Sustainable?

Sustainability in commercial landscaping isn't just good ethics. It's increasingly a tenant demand. A CBRE Research (2024) survey found that 73% of commercial tenants consider a property's sustainability practices when making leasing decisions. Smart maintenance programs address this directly.

A 2024 CBRE Research survey found that 73 percent of commercial tenants consider a property's sustainability practices when making leasing decisions, making sustainable landscape maintenance a competitive advantage for property managers.

Water Conservation

Smart irrigation controllers, drip irrigation for planting beds, and rainwater harvesting systems all reduce water consumption. For Northern Virginia properties, converting portions of turf to native plantings like Virginia sweetspire, Eastern red columbine, or switchgrass cuts irrigation needs by 30-50% in those areas.

Reduced Chemical Inputs

Integrated Pest Management (IPM) programs minimize pesticide use by focusing on prevention rather than treatment. Many Reston and Great Falls properties have adopted IPM approaches in response to community preferences for reduced chemical exposure near walking trails and playgrounds.

Electric and Low-Emission Equipment

Battery-powered mowers and blowers are becoming viable for commercial properties. They're quieter (important for office parks and medical campuses) and produce zero direct emissions. While upfront equipment costs are higher, fuel and maintenance savings often offset the difference within two to three years.

: Sustainability upgrades don't have to happen all at once. We've found that converting just 15-20% of a property's high-maintenance turf areas to native plantings creates noticeable water savings while keeping the overall appearance that tenants and visitors expect.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should commercial properties be mowed in Northern Virginia?

During the active growing season (April through October), most Northern Virginia commercial properties need weekly mowing. The Virginia Cooperative Extension recommends maintaining tall fescue, the region's dominant cool-season grass, at 3 to 3.5 inches. During peak summer heat, biweekly mowing at a higher cut protects turf health.

What's the average cost of a commercial landscape maintenance contract?

According to BOMA, Mid-Atlantic commercial properties spend $3,000 to $12,000 per acre annually. For a typical 5-acre Dulles corridor office park, expect $20,000 to $40,000 per year for comprehensive maintenance including seasonal color and basic snow removal. HOA common areas in Ashburn and Brambleton tend to fall at the lower end of that range.

Should snow removal be part of a landscape maintenance contract?

Including snow removal in your landscape maintenance contract provides two advantages: bundled pricing and guaranteed priority. Properties that contract separately for snow removal often find themselves lower on the vendor's priority list during major storms. In Northern Virginia, where ice events can be more disruptive than snow, having a single vendor manage both landscape and winter services improves response time.

How do you measure landscape maintenance quality?

Establish measurable performance standards in your contract. Common metrics include: turf color and density ratings, response time to service requests, plant survival rates for seasonal installations, and monthly property inspection scores. The CAI recommends quarterly formal evaluations with your vendor, plus monthly walkthroughs.

When should you switch landscape maintenance vendors?

Consider switching when you see consistent missed visits, declining turf or plant health over two consecutive seasons, unresolved safety issues, or poor communication. However, the IFMA data showing a 20-30% cost increase during vendor transitions means switching should be a last resort. Try formal corrective action first.
