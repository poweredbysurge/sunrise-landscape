What Does Landscape Design Cost on Its Own?

Design-only services — where a landscape designer or architect creates plans without performing the installation — are less common in the residential market than most homeowners expect. Most Northern Virginia landscape firms offer design as part of a design-build package, where the design fee is credited toward installation.

Standalone design fees vary significantly based on scope:

  • Concept design (hand sketch or digital rendering of general direction): $500–$1,500
  • Full residential design plan (detailed planting plan, layout, materials specification): $1,500–$5,000
  • Large property or complex site (drainage issues, significant grading, multi-zone design): $3,000–$8,000+

If a firm credits your design fee toward installation, that's a reasonable arrangement and common in the industry. If they charge for design without any path toward installation credit, make sure the deliverable is detailed enough to give to a separate contractor for bidding — you should receive permit-ready drawings with plant lists and material specifications, not just a pretty rendering.

Full Landscape Design-Build Costs

This is what most homeowners are actually budgeting for — design plus installation performed by the same firm. Here are honest ranges for Northern Virginia.

Focused Projects ($10,000–$25,000)

A single well-defined scope: front yard re-landscaping, a new patio, a planting overhaul of existing beds, or a targeted drainage fix. These projects typically involve one trade discipline (grading, hardscape, or planting) rather than a combination.

Examples in this range:

  • New patio installation, 400–600 sq ft natural stone or pavers: $12,000–$22,000
  • Front yard landscape renovation with new plantings and mulch: $8,000–$18,000
  • Retaining wall with integrated planting: $10,000–$20,000
  • Drainage solution with regrading: $8,000–$15,000

Mid-Range Projects ($25,000–$75,000)

Projects that combine hardscape and planting, or tackle a complete zone of the property (full backyard, full front yard plus entry). The investment level where design quality becomes a significant differentiator — a well-designed mid-range project looks as impressive as a cheaply executed high-budget one.

Examples in this range:

  • Backyard outdoor living space with patio, seating wall, and planting: $30,000–$55,000
  • Complete front and side yard redesign with hardscape elements: $25,000–$45,000
  • Patio plus fire pit plus landscape bed renovation: $35,000–$60,000

Comprehensive Transformations ($75,000–$200,000+)

Full property redesigns on larger Northern Virginia lots — the kind of project in Great Falls or McLean that touches every outdoor zone, involves multiple hardscape features, mature plantings, outdoor kitchens, lighting, water features, or significant grading work.

What Affects the Cost of a Landscape Project?

Hardscape vs. Planting Ratio

Hardscape — patios, walls, walkways, fire features — is significantly more expensive per square foot than planted areas. Natural stone runs $25–$50+ per square foot installed. Concrete pavers run $15–$35 per square foot. Planting beds at scale run $8–$20 per square foot depending on plant sizes and density.

A project that is 60% hardscape will cost considerably more than one of the same footprint that is 60% planting. When you're comparing estimates, make sure you're comparing equivalent hardscape-to-planting ratios.

Site Conditions

Northern Virginia's clay-heavy soils create drainage challenges that add cost to almost any landscape project. Clay doesn't drain efficiently. Water pools. Roots struggle. These conditions are manageable with proper soil amendment, drainage infrastructure, and the right plant selection — but that work has a cost that shouldn't surprise you in a well-scoped proposal.

Slope, existing tree roots, underground utilities, and HOA restrictions are additional site factors that affect labor time and therefore cost. A flat, clear property costs less to work on than a sloped lot with mature trees and an HOA that requires landscape committee approval.

Material Selection

Bluestone costs more than concrete pavers. Custom-cut natural stone costs more than standard modular stone. Larger caliper trees cost more than smaller specimens. These choices ripple through a project budget in ways that aren't always obvious when reviewing an estimate. A good contractor walks you through material options with honest cost context so you can make informed trade-offs.

Seasonality

Spring and early summer are the highest-demand periods for Northern Virginia landscape contractors. Booking and executing projects in late summer, fall, or even early spring (when soil is workable but before peak demand) can sometimes allow more scheduling flexibility and faster start dates. Material costs don't vary significantly by season, but scheduling can.

What Does a Landscape Project Return at Resale?

The National Association of Realtors regularly surveys real estate professionals on landscape investment returns. Current data shows:

  • Standard lawn care and landscape maintenance: 100%+ recovery at resale (dollar-for-dollar return)
  • Landscape renovation (new plantings, design): 70–100% recovery
  • Outdoor living features (patio, fire pit, outdoor kitchen): 50–80% recovery
  • Overall landscape investment: homes with quality professional landscaping sell faster and command higher per-square-foot prices

In the Great Falls, McLean, and Vienna markets specifically, where buyers are sophisticated and expectations for outdoor spaces are high, quality landscaping is frequently cited as a significant factor in both buyer decision-making and final sale price. It's not guaranteed return, but it's real.

How to Read a Landscape Estimate

A professional landscape estimate should include line items for materials, labor, and equipment separately — not a single lump-sum number with no breakdown. You should be able to see what you're paying for hardscape materials, what labor is allocated to installation, and what the design or project management fee covers.

Watch for:

  • Allowances instead of specifics: "Allowance for stone: $3,000" is not a real price. It means the contractor hasn't priced the stone yet. Get actual material specifications and prices before signing.
  • Scope that's vague around site conditions: "Site prep as needed" should be defined. What does site prep include? Is regrading included? Rock removal? Debris hauling?
  • No payment schedule tied to milestones: A legitimate contractor structures payments around project milestones — design approval, material delivery, installation completion, final walkthrough. Large upfront deposits without a schedule are a red flag.
